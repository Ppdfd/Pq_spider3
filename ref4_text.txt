ABSTRACT The proliferation of Internet of Things (IoT) devices in cloud-assisted environments raises
urgent concerns regarding fine-grained access control, policy privacy, and resilience against quantum
adversaries. To address these challenges, we propose a lightweight and quantum-resistant access control
framework that combines Ring-LWE-based Ciphertext-Policy Attribute-Based Encryption (CP-ABE) with
a fog-assisted split-path partial encryption architecture. In our scheme, IoT devices encrypt raw data using
AES-256-GCM, while fog nodes perform the RLWE-CP-ABE encapsulation of session keys, thereby
reducing device-side computation while ensuring that neither plaintext nor session keys are exposed
to semi-trusted fog nodes. To preserve policy confidentiality, we integrate a salted attribute-hashing
mechanism into the Linear Secret Sharing Scheme (LSSS) matrices, concealing access policies even during
fog-side processing. Furthermore, we design an epoch-based and blacklist-based revocation mechanism
that supports both user-level and attribute-level revocation without requiring ciphertext re-encryption.
Security is guaranteed through CRYSTALS-Dilithium signatures and Poly1305 message authentication,
which provide post-quantum authenticity and integrity. Experimental results confirm that our framework
significantly reduces computational and communication overhead while achieving scalable, verifiable,
and post-quantum-secure access control–outperforming existing lattice-based CP-ABE schemes in both
efficiency and security.
INDEX TERMS IoT cloud, CP-ABE, post-quantum cryptography, lattice-based cryptography, RLWE, fog
computing, access control, CRYSTALS-Dilithium.
I. INTRODUCTION
T
HE INTERNET of Things (IoT) is fundamentally trans-
forming critical infrastructures by enabling pervasive
connectivity and autonomous data exchange among billions
of devices across sectors such as healthcare, transporta-
tion, smart cities, and industrial control systems. These
interconnected devices continuously generate vast volumes
of sensitive data, which is offloaded to distributed fog
and cloud infrastructures for scalable storage and real-time
analytics.
However, securing this data in large-scale, multi-
tenant environments remains a significant challenge. This
problem is intensified by the emerging threat of quantum
computing.
Conventional access control mechanisms in IoT systems
predominantly rely on classical cryptographic primitives,
including RSA, ECC, and Ciphertext-Policy Attribute-Based
Encryption (CP-ABE). Despite their widespread use, these
schemes are vulnerable to quantum adversaries due to the
underlying hardness assumptions being susceptible to Shor’s
and Grover’s algorithms. In contrast, post-quantum cryptog-
raphy (PQC), particularly schemes grounded in the Learning
With Errors (LWE) and Ring-LWE problems [1], [2], [3],
has emerged as a promising alternative. Among them,
lattice-based CP-ABE provides a powerful foundation for
fine-grained access control by embedding attribute policies
directly into ciphertexts, ensuring that only authorized users
can decrypt the data.
Nonetheless, existing lattice-based CP-ABE
schemes [4], [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [30] are still not
suitable for deployment in resource-constrained IoT-cloud
environments due to several unresolved problems:

1) Excessive Computational and Communication
Overhead: High-dimensional polynomial operations
result in prohibitive costs for encryption, decryption,
and key generation. Combined with bulky ciphertext
structures, this overhead makes lattice CP-ABE
impractical for IoT devices with limited computing
power and for bandwidth-constrained IoT networks.
2) Absence of Secure F og Delegation: While fog comput-
ing offers natural opportunities to offload computation,
existing lattice CP-ABE frameworks lack secure mech-
anisms that allow fog nodes to assist with partial
decryption or policy enforcement without compromis-
ing data confidentiality.
3) Policy Exposure and Privacy Risks: Many CP-ABE
constructions embed access structures in plaintext,
enabling adversaries or semi-trusted fog nodes to infer
user attributes, which can lead to profiling attacks or
leakage of sensitive access patterns.
4) Inefficient and Incomplete Revocation Current revo-
cation mechanisms [6], [14], [15], [17], [25], [31]
often require ciphertext re-encryption or system-wide
key redistribution, which are computationally expen-
sive and disruptive. Moreover, most schemes focus
only on attribute-level revocation, neglecting user-level
revocation and forward secrecy.
Recent efforts have attempted to mitigate subsets of
these challenges. Hybrid encryption models [11], [12], [13]
leverage symmetric encryption with lattice-based key encap-
sulation to reduce device-side overhead but fall short in
enabling fog-enforced, fine-grained access control. Similarly,
revocable lattice-based CP-ABE constructions [6], [14],
[15] introduce mediator-based revocation, yet lack support
for dynamic policy hiding and real-time delegation. Post-
quantum authentication solutions [16], [17] address data
integrity but overlook policy enforcement and access coordi-
nation. Critically, no existing framework provides a unified
solution that integrates quantum-resilient encryption, policy
privacy, fine-grained and forward-secure revocation, and fog-
assisted secure access enforcement.
To address these gaps, we propose a fog-assisted,
quantum-resilient, and revocation-aware access control
framework tailored for secure IoT data sharing. To the best
of our knowledge, our paper is the first to provide a unified
solution for secure and fine-grained lattice CP-ABE access
control in Fog-assisted IoT cloud. The proposed system
introduces the following core innovations:
1) Split-Path Partial Encryption with F og-Assisted
Encapsulation: We introduce an architectural refine-
ment in which IoT devices perform only lightweight
AES-256-GCM encryption of raw data and generate
salted attribute hashes, while fog nodes complete the
RLWE-based CP-ABE encapsulation of session keys.
This split-path design significantly reduces device-
side computational overhead, ensures that neither
plaintext nor session keys are revealed to semi-trusted
fog nodes, and naturally integrates with our policy-
hiding LSSS construction and epoch/blacklist-based
revocation mechanism.
2) Policy-Hiding Attribute Encoding: We introduce salted
hash encoding of attribute labels embedded into LSSS
matrices, which conceals the underlying policy even
from fog nodes. This prevents inference and profiling
attacks while retaining the expressiveness of fine-
grained access structures.
3) F orward-Secure, Fine-Grained Revocation: We pro-
pose an efficient revocation mechanism that combines
epoch-based key expiration with blacklist verifica-
tion. This dual strategy enables both user-level and
attribute-level revocation while eliminating the costly
ciphertext re-encryption process and maintaining for-
ward security.
4) Post-Quantum Authenticity and Integrity: We integrate
CRYSTALS-Dilithium signatures and Poly1305 mes-
sage authentication codes to guarantee authenticity and
integrity in a post-quantum environment, preventing
tampering during fog-side delegation and transforma-
tion.
5) Comprehensive Performance Evaluation: We imple-
ment the proposed framework in a Raspberry
Pi–fog–cloud testbed and evaluate its performance
against existing lattice-based CP-ABE baselines.
Results demonstrate significant reductions in device-
side computation and communication overhead, as well
as scalability in revocation management and policy
privacy preservation.

II. RELATED WORK
Securing data access in resource-constrained IoT environ-
ments requires cryptographic schemes that are not only
lightweight and fine-grained but also resilient against emerg-
ing quantum threats. This section reviews relevant works
across three main domains: (i) classical CP-ABE schemes
tailored for IoT, (ii) post-quantum lattice-based CP-ABE
schemes, and (iii) supporting techniques for outsourcing,
revocation, and authentication.
A. LIGHTWEIGHT AND FOG-ASSISTED CP-ABE FOR IoT
To alleviate the computational load on IoT devices, sev-
eral classical CP-ABE enhancements have been proposed.
Tan et al. [18] introduced a lightweight KP-ABE scheme
to minimize pairing operations, making it suitable for
constrained devices. Miao et al. [19] and Yin et al. [20]
proposed CP-ABE schemes combined with searchable
encryption (SE) and fog computing. These designs effec-
tively offload most computational tasks to edge nodes.

quantum adversaries.
B. POST-QUANTUM LATTICE-BASED CP-ABE
To address quantum threats, recent studies have explored
lattice-based CP-ABE constructions rooted in the LWE
and Ring-LWE problems. Qian and Wu [16] and
Sravya et al. [5] proposed Module-LWE-based schemes that
emphasize lightweight tree structures and secret key reuse to
reduce decryption complexity in IoT. Zhang et al. [6] intro-
duced a novel puncturable Ciphertext-Policy Attribute-Based
Encryption (Pt-CP-ABE) scheme founded on the LWE
assumption. This scheme allows data owners to specify fine-
grained access policies for encrypted data. Additionally, once
a ciphertext is decrypted, users can autonomously update
their private keys and revoke access to previously accessible
ciphertexts by puncturing the associated tags embedded
in the ciphertext. Fun and Samsudin [24] presented a
Ring-LWE-based CP-ABE leveraging Linear Secret Sharing
Schemes (LSSS) with randomized keys to achieve collusion
resistance. Y ang et al. [25] and Huang et al. [14] developed
revocable CP-ABE schemes supporting multi-authority mod-
els and attribute-level revocation, but they do not support
fog-assisted processing or policy privacy. Y ao et al. [2] and
Datta et al. [3] introduced decentralized multi-authority CP-
ABE schemes under the LWE assumption, enabling authority
flexibility and collusion resistance.
Despite these advances, most existing schemes lack
support for secure delegation, policy hiding, and efficient
ciphertext transformation—capabilities essential for scalable
and privacy-preserving access control in fog-assisted IoT
settings. In [29], Xu et al. proposed a more efficient and
quantum-resistant encryption scheme called Ring-ExpLWE,
which replaces the binary error distribution used in Ring-
BinLWE with an exponential distribution. To assess its
security, they analyzed the scheme’s resistance to quantum
hybrid attacks by measuring runtime and comparing the stan-
dard deviation of the noise polynomial coefficients. Results
show that Ring-ExpLWE incurs a higher computational cost
for quantum hybrid attacks and exhibits a more dispersed
noise distribution than Ring-BinLWE, indicating stronger
security under equivalent parameters.
C. REVOCATION, POLICY HIDING, OUTSOURCING, AND
AUTHENTICATION ENHANCEMENTS
Several works have explored revocation, policy hiding, out-
sourcing, and authentication in post-quantum access control.
Revocation Mechanisms: Zhao et al. [17] proposed RL-
ABE, a lattice-based scheme supporting attribute revocation
through rekeying and ciphertext updates. However, it suf-
fers from high revocation overhead and lacks user-level
granularity. Huang et al. [14] designed a revocable ABE
resilient to key exposure, though it does not address fog
delegation or policy privacy. Wen et al. [15] developed
LaRRS for vehicular networks using revocable ring sig-
natures, prioritizing anonymity rather than fine-grained
access control. Zhang et al. [6] employed puncturable
encryption to enable self-revocation by updating keys after
decryption, which is efficient; however, it does not provide
centralized enforcement or fog-assisted processing. More
recently, Chen et al. [32] presented a revocable attribute-
based encryption (RABE-DI) scheme that guarantees both
user revocation and verifiable data integrity, enabling users
to detect incorrect cloud-side revocations. While efficient
within cloud environments, the scheme’s reliance on bilinear
pairings still incurs high computational cost, making it less
suitable for constrained IoT devices.
Policy-Hiding and Updating: Protecting the confidentiality
of access policies has been another important research
direction. Gu et al. [33] proposed EABE-PUFPH, which
achieves full policy hiding and reliable policy updating,
ensuring that access structures remain confidential even when
policies evolve. Similarly, Li et al. [34] designed PH-MG-
ABE, a flexible multi-group policy-hidden ABE scheme that
allows dynamic user grouping and hidden access structures,
making it suitable for collaborative cloud environments.
These works demonstrate the feasibility of maintaining
strong confidentiality of access structures, yet they do not
integrate efficient revocation mechanisms tailored to IoT-fog
environments and lack post-quantum resilience.
Outsourcing Techniques: Bagchi et al. [4] integrated
trapdoor sampling and Shamir’s secret sharing in a Ring-
LWE multi-authority CP-ABE scheme to support partial
delegation. They later extended this work [9] for blockchain-
based healthcare auditing, but the revocation model remained
rigid and imposed heavy key-management costs on users.
Xu et al. [29] introduced Ring-ExpLWE to enhance resis-
tance against quantum-hybrid attacks, yet their design lacked
revocation support and fog delegation capabilities.
Authentication Enhancements: Post-quantum authentica-
tion has also attracted attention. Shen et al. [26] and
Wang et al. [27] optimized CRYSTALS-Dilithium for
GPU and FPGA platforms, while Lu et al. [28] built
lightweight modules for Kyber and Dilithium on embedded
systems. Although these works strengthen authentication
efficiency, they are not tailored for fine-grained access
control and offer no mechanisms for revocation or attribute
enforcement.
Despite various advancements, no existing scheme simul-
taneously supports post-quantum security, fine-grained and
privacy-preserving access policies, flexible and user-level
revocation, and fog-assisted lightweight delegation tailored
for IoT-cloud environments.

III. PRELIMINARIES
This section outlines the fundamental cryptographic tools and
assumptions that underpin the proposed scheme, including
Ring-LWE hardness, trapdoor sampling via SamplePre, and
Linear Secret Sharing Schemes (LSSS) for fine-grained
access control.
A. RING LEARNING WITH ERRORS (RING-LWE)
The Ring-LWE assumption is a post-quantum hard problem
widely used in lattice-based cryptography. Let Rq =
Zq[x]/(f(x)) be a polynomial ring where f(x) is typically a
cyclotomic polynomial.
The Ring-LWE problem states that, given samples of the
form (a(x), b(x) = a(x)s(x) + e(x)) where a(x) ∈ Rq is
uniformly random, s(x), e(x) ← χ are sampled from an error
distribution, it is computationally hard to distinguish such
samples from uniformly random pairs in Rq × Rq.
The hardness of Ring-LWE has been reduced to worst-
case problems on ideal lattices such as GapSVP and SIVP .
In our scheme, Ring-LWE underpins the security of
ciphertext-policy ABE operations and session key encapsu-
lation.
B. TRAPDOOR SAMPLING AND SAMPLEPRE ALGORITHM
To securely support lattice-based key generation and pol-
icy enforcement, we utilize trapdoor sampling via the
SamplePre algorithm introduced by Micciancio and
Peikert. This algorithm allows the generation of small-norm
solutions to linear equations over a ring, which is crucial for
attribute-bound key issuance.

In our scheme, we use SamplePre to derive cipher-

hashed attribute labels, ensuring security under the Ring-SIS
assumption.
C. LINEAR SECRET SHARING SCHEME (LSSS)
An LSSS is used to express access structures in CP-ABE.
Formally, an access policy is encoded as a matrix M ∈ Zℓ×t
q ,
where each row corresponds to an attribute, and a mapping
ρ :[ ℓ] → U associates matrix rows with attributes from the
universe U .
A secret value s ∈ Z
q is shared by constructing a random
vector ⃗v = (s, r2,..., rt)T , and shares are computed as λi =
Mi ·⃗v. A user whose attribute set satisfies the policy can
reconstruct s via a linear combination of λi values. This
guarantees that only authorized sets of attributes recover s,
while unauthorized sets obtain no useful information.
In our scheme, we enhance LSSS with privacy-preserving
hashing. Each attribute label is first hashed with a random
salt and then mapped to a row in the LSSS matrix,
thereby hiding the access structure while preserving its
functionality.

A. SYSTEM MODEL
Our system model (Fig. 1) comprises seven main entities:
1) Trusted Authority (TA) is a fully trusted setup entity
responsible for system initialization. It generates global
public parameters and master secret keys using lattice
trapdoor functions to provide post-quantum resilience.
The TA issues partial attribute-based secret keys to
end users, embedding revocation and policy metadata,
and trapdoor keys for fog nodes to support partial
decryption and access enforcement.
2) IoT Devices are microcontroller-based sensors or actu-
ators that collect raw environmental or operational
data. Due to limited resources, each IoT device
performs lightweight partial encryption, embedding
minimal metadata and offloading expensive crypto-
graphic operations to fog nodes.
3) F og Nodes are responsible for completing partial
encryption, performing delegated decryption, and
enforcing revocation policies in coordination with the
Trusted Authority. In addition, they offload com-
putationally intensive lattice operations and digital
signature verification from IoT devices, ensuring that
only lightweight tasks remain at the edge.
4) Dispatcher is a logically centralized entity that oper-
ates in both encryption and decryption phases. It
selects suitable fog nodes for completing encryption
based on load, proximity, or trust, and routes user
decryption requests while enforcing revocation and
access policies. Although it does not access plaintext
or keys, the Dispatcher plays a critical role in secure
coordination, with all sensitive messages (e.g., epoch
updates) digitally signed by the TA and verified by
fog nodes.
5) IoT Cloud Storage: A semi-trusted storage entity that
stores fully encrypted data packets. While the cloud
ensures availability and scalability, it is assumed to
be honest-but-curious and cannot decrypt or learn data
contents. It may also maintain indexed metadata for
packet retrieval.
6) End Users are authorized users—such as data analysts,
system operators, or third-party auditors—who receive
partial attribute-based secret keys from the TA. If
their attributes satisfy the ciphertext policy and they
are not revoked, users can obtain partially decrypted
ciphertexts from fog nodes and complete the final
decryption locally.
